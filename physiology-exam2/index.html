Sensory Systems

Stimulus → Action Potential

  1. Stimulus hits receptor
  2. Receptor potential generated (graded)
  3. Threshold reached → AP fires in afferent neuron
  4. Signal travels to spinal cord / brainstem
  5. Relay through thalamus → primary cortex

Lateral Inhibition

Neighboring neurons inhibit each other via interneurons. The most-stimulated neuron suppresses its neighbors → sharpens contrast & spatial resolution (e.g., two-point discrimination on skin).

Eye Structure & Light Path

Rods 120M — low light, peripheral, no color
Cones 6M — color, concentrated at fovea
Phototransduction Light → bleaches rhodopsin → hyperpolarizes photoreceptor (opposite of most!) → less glutamate → ON bipolar depolarizes
Focusing Ciliary muscles relax → lens flattens (far). Contract → lens rounds (near). Pupil constricts in bright light via iris sphincter.

Cochlear Membrane & Hearing

Sound path Sound → tympanic membrane → ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) → oval window → fluid waves → basilar membrane deflects → hair cells bend → K⁺ influx → depolarization → AP → CN VIII → auditory cortex
Why K⁺? Endolymph is K⁺-rich (unlike most ECF). Hair cells depolarize via K⁺ influx (not Na⁺), maintained by stria vascularis which requires robust blood flow.
Vestibular Semicircular canals (rotation) → cupula deflects → hair cells. Utricle/saccule (linear accel) → otolith moves → hair cells.

Taste Receptor Cells

Type ISupport/glial-like cells; salt sensing via ion channels
Type IIGPCRs; detect sweet, bitter, umami → IP3 pathway → ATP release
Type IIISynapse-forming cells; sour (H⁺) & salt; release serotonin

Olfactory Structure

  • Olfactory receptor neurons — bipolar; cilia extend into mucus; direct axons to olfactory bulb (only CN I)
  • Basal/stem cells at the base → regenerate receptor neurons (unique!)
  • Horizontal cells — sustentacular support, spread laterally to nourish epithelium
  • Pathway: olfactory bulb → piriform cortex (bypasses thalamus)

Peripheral Receptor Types

  • Mechanoreceptors — touch, pressure, vibration, proprioception
  • Thermoreceptors — temperature
  • Nociceptors — pain / damage
  • Photoreceptors — light
  • Chemoreceptors — chemicals (taste, smell, O₂/CO₂)
  • Polymodal receptors — respond to multiple stimuli (e.g., C-fiber nociceptors respond to heat, chemicals, mechanical damage)

Muscle Fatigue Causes

  • Pi (inorganic phosphate) accumulation → inhibits myosin ATPase & reduces Ca²⁺ sensitivity
  • H⁺ accumulation (lactic acid lowers pH) → inhibits glycolytic enzymes
  • K⁺ accumulation outside cell → reduces membrane excitability
  • ATP depletion → cross-bridges can't detach
  • Central fatigue (CNS reduces motor drive)

Neuromuscular Adaptations (Strength without Hypertrophy)

  • Increased motor unit recruitment
  • Better synchronization of motor units
  • Reduced co-contraction of antagonists
  • Improved neuromuscular efficiency
  • Decreased inhibition from Golgi Tendon Organs

Key Terms

  • Golgi Tendon Organ — detects muscle tension; inhibits contraction to prevent tendon damage (autogenic inhibition)
  • Lactate Threshold — exercise intensity where lactate production outpaces clearance; marked by rise in blood lactate
  • DOMS — delayed onset muscle soreness; peaks 24–72 hrs post exercise; from eccentric damage & inflammation, NOT lactic acid
  • Hypertrophy — satellite cell activation → myonuclei donation → larger myofibrils; stimulated by mechanical tension + metabolic stress

Blood & Circulation

Blood Composition

Erythropoiesis Kidneys sense low O₂ → secrete EPO → stimulates bone marrow → RBC production increases. High altitude, anemia, bleeding all trigger EPO release.
Albumin functions (1) Maintains oncotic pressure (keeps fluid in vessels) (2) Carrier protein for hormones, drugs, fatty acids. Produced more in protein deficiency states.
Blood distribution at rest vs exercise Rest: most blood in veins (venous reservoir). Exercise: redistributed to working muscles via sympathetic vasoconstriction of non-essential beds + vasodilation in muscle.

Kidney & Blood Volume

  • Low BP → JGA releases renin → angiotensin I → ACE → angiotensin II
  • Ang II → vasoconstriction + stimulates aldosterone
  • Aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption → water follows → ↑ blood volume
  • ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide) opposes this when volume too high

Anemia Types

  • Iron deficiency — small, pale RBCs (microcytic)
  • B12/folate deficiency — large RBCs (macrocytic/megaloblastic)
  • Hemolytic — RBCs destroyed too fast
  • Aplastic — bone marrow failure
  • Sickle cell — abnormal Hb shape → cell deforms, clogs vessels

Heart & Cardiac Output

Action Potentials: Ventricular Myocyte vs Pacemaker Cell

Key difference Ventricular cells have a stable resting potential (~−90mV), big fast Na⁺ spike, and long plateau (phase 2, Ca²⁺). Pacemaker cells have NO stable rest — "funny current" (Hcn channels) spontaneously depolarizes them. Ca²⁺ channels (not Na⁺) drive upstroke.
Conduction path SA node → AV node (delay) → Bundle of His → Bundle branches → Purkinje fibers → ventricular myocytes
Autonomic tone Sympathetic (NE/β1) increases HR (steeper pacemaker slope). Parasympathetic (ACh/M2) decreases HR (hyperpolarizes, reduces slope). "Autonomic tone" = balance at rest = slight vagal dominance.

Starling Forces at Capillaries

Edema Occurs when net filtration > lymphatic drainage. Causes: ↑ capillary hydrostatic P (heart failure), ↓ plasma oncotic P (liver disease / low albumin), lymphatic obstruction (elephantiasis — Wuchereria bancrofti blocks lymphatics).
Frank-Starling Law Increased venous return (preload) → more stretch of ventricle → stronger contraction. Heart automatically matches output to input.

Cardiac Muscle Features

  • Intercalated discs with gap junctions → electrical syncytium
  • Long plateau (phase 2) → long refractory period → no tetanus possible
  • Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release (CICR) from SR
  • Highly aerobic; many mitochondria
  • Cannot regenerate after injury

Respiratory System

Hemoglobin O₂ Dissociation Curve (Interactive)

Cooperative binding Hb has 4 subunits. First O₂ binds, changes shape → easier for others to bind. Sigmoidal curve = cooperativity.
Bohr effect ↑CO₂ / ↓pH / ↑temp / ↑2,3-DPG → right shift → Hb releases O₂ more easily (great at exercising muscles).
CO₂ transport (1) 70% as bicarbonate (CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻, catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase in RBCs) (2) 23% bound to Hb as carbaminohemoglobin (3) 7% dissolved in plasma.
High altitude Low PO₂ → ↑EPO → ↑RBCs; ↑2,3-DPG (right shifts curve); hyperventilation (↓CO₂, alkalosis); over weeks: more mitochondria, more capillaries.

  • CO₂ is the primary driver of breathing (more sensitive than O₂)

  2. Primary bronchi

Trachea & Esophagus

  • Trachea has C-shaped cartilage rings
  • Open side of "C" faces posteriorly (toward esophagus)
  • Allows esophagus to expand when swallowing food
  • Explains why swallowing briefly affects breathing
  • Conducting zone: moves air; no gas exchange
  • Respiratory zone (bronchioles → alveoli): gas exchange occurs

Ventilation Mechanics & Gas Laws

Boyle's Law & Breathing

  • Boyle's Law: Pressure ∝ 1/Volume (at constant temperature)
  • Inspiration: Diaphragm contracts & flattens → thoracic volume ↑ → pressure ↓ → air flows IN
  • Expiration: Diaphragm relaxes → elastic recoil → volume ↓ → pressure ↑ → air flows OUT
  • Forced expiration: internal intercostals + abdominals assist
  • Phrenic nerve (C3–C5) controls diaphragm
  • Medulla controls automatic breathing rhythm

Compliance & Surfactant

  • Compliance — how easily lungs stretch
  • Elasticity — recoil tendency after stretch
  • Lungs resist inflation due to elastic tissue + surface tension in alveoli
  • Surfactant (from Type II pneumocytes) reduces alveolar surface tension
  • Law of Laplace: P = 2T/r — smaller alveoli have higher collapse pressure; surfactant counters this
  • Premature infants lack surfactant → infant RDS

Dalton's Law & Partial Pressure

  • Total atmospheric pressure at sea level: 760 mmHg
  • Air composition: N₂ ~78%, O₂ ~21%, Ar ~1%, CO₂ trace
  • Partial pressure = fraction of gas × total pressure
  • O₂ partial pressure at sea level: ~159 mmHg
  • At high altitude: total pressure falls → PO₂ falls → less O₂ available
  • Gas exchange driven by partial pressure gradients, not percentages

Henry's Law & Gas Dissolution

  • Amount of gas dissolved in blood depends on: solubility, temperature, partial pressure
  • Higher PO₂ → more O₂ dissolves into blood
  • O₂ moves: atmosphere → alveoli → blood → tissues (each step lower PO₂)
  • CO₂ moves in reverse: tissues → blood → lungs (exhaled)
  • ~97% of O₂ carried by hemoglobin; ~3% dissolved in plasma

CO₂ as Breathing Regulator

  • Breathing is regulated primarily by CO₂ levels, not O₂
  • High CO₂ / low pH → ↑ ventilation
  • Low CO₂ / high pH → ↓ ventilation
  • Shallow water drowning: hyperventilation before diving → CO₂ drops → brain thinks O₂ is fine → swimmer holds breath too long → O₂ silently falls → unconsciousness underwater without warning
  • This is why competitive breath-holding with prior hyperventilation is dangerous

High Altitude & Deep Sea Diving

  • High altitude: ↓ PO₂ → hyperventilation → ↑ EPO → ↑ RBCs; ↑ 2,3-DPG; more capillaries; improved O₂ extraction (Tibetan/Andean populations adapted genetically)
  • Air pollution: PM2.5 (<2.5 µm) and PM10 (<10 µm) penetrate deep into lungs → inflammation, cardiovascular disease, ↑ mortality near freeways
  • Decompression sickness ("the bends"): N₂ dissolves into blood under deep-sea pressure → rapid ascent → N₂ forms bubbles → joint pain, neurological damage
  • Prevention: slow ascent; decompression stops; decompression chambers

Immune System

Innate vs Adaptive Immunity

Innate (Fast, Non-specific)

  • Neutrophils — first responders, phagocytosis
  • Macrophages — phagocytosis + cytokines + antigen presentation
  • Dendritic cells — link innate to adaptive; present antigens
  • NK cells — kill virus-infected & tumor cells
  • TLRs/NLRs — pattern recognition receptors for PAMPs
  • Complement — opsonization, lysis, inflammation

Adaptive (Slow, Specific, Memory)

  • T cells — cell-mediated; need MHC + antigen
  • B cells — humoral; make antibodies
  • Helper T (CD4) — activate B cells + cytotoxic T via cytokines
  • Cytotoxic T (CD8) — kill infected cells (MHC I)
  • Memory cells — persist long-term → faster response on re-exposure
  • MHC I — on all nucleated cells; MHC II — on APCs

Complement System

  • 3 pathways (classical, lectin, alternative) → all converge at C3
  • C3b → opsonization (tags pathogen for phagocytosis)
  • C5a → chemotaxis & inflammation
  • MAC (membrane attack complex) → punches holes in pathogen membranes

Inflammation Effects

  • Vasodilation → redness & heat
  • ↑ capillary permeability → swelling (edema)
  • Pain from prostaglandins + bradykinin on nociceptors
  • Cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α) → systemic fever, acute phase proteins

Lymph Nodes

  • Filter lymph fluid
  • Site where T & B cells encounter antigens presented by dendritic cells
  • Germinal centers: B cell maturation & affinity maturation
  • Swollen nodes = active immune response

Immune Memory

  1. Antigen activates naive T/B cells
  2. Clonal expansion — many copies made
  3. Effector cells fight infection
  4. Contraction phase — most cells die
  5. Memory cells persist → long-lived, quick to respond
  6. 2nd exposure → faster + stronger response (basis of vaccination)

Innate Immunity — Pattern Recognition & Early Response

PRRs & PAMPs

  • PRRs (Pattern Recognition Receptors) — detect common microbial signatures
  • PAMPs (Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns) — conserved molecular patterns on microbes (e.g., LPS, flagellin, viral RNA)
  • Key PRRs: Toll-like receptors (TLRs), NOD-like receptors (NLRs)
  • When PRRs detect PAMPs → release chemokines (attract immune cells) and cytokines (regulate immune response)
  • This triggers the early inflammatory cascade

Phagocytosis & Cell Hierarchy

  • Neutrophils — first responders; arrive quickly; phagocytose and destroy pathogens; short-lived
  • Monocytes/Macrophages — arrive later; longer-term cleanup crew; also present antigens
  • Process: engulf pathogen → phagosome fuses with lysosome → digest and destroy
  • Diapedesis — leukocytes squeeze through blood vessel walls into tissues (guided by chemokines)
  • NK cells — kill virus-infected cells and tumor cells using perforins and granzymes (innate)

Fever & Interferons

  • Cytokines IL-1, IL-6, TNF → act on hypothalamus → fever
  • Fever also causes sleepiness and ↓ iron in blood (making environment hostile to pathogens)
  • Interferons — released by virus-infected cells; warn neighboring cells to activate antiviral defenses
  • Mast cells — release histamine on activation → vasodilation + ↑ vascular permeability → classic inflammation signs (redness, heat, swelling)
  • Mast cells also key in allergy (IgE binding)

Immunological Tolerance

  • Prevents immune system from attacking self-antigens
  • Clonal deletion — self-reactive cells destroyed in thymus (negative selection)
  • Clonal anergy — self-reactive cells inactivated (no co-stimulation)
  • Regulatory T cells (Tregs) — suppress autoreactive immune cells; secrete IL-10, TGF-β
  • Failure of tolerance → autoimmunity

Immune Dysfunction — Autoimmunity, Allergy & Tumor Immunology

Autoimmunity

  • Immune system attacks self — failure of tolerance
  • Mechanisms:
    • Hidden self-antigens becoming exposed
    • Altered self-antigens (drug/infection modification)
    • Molecular mimicry — cross-reactivity between foreign and self-antigens
  • Examples: Rheumatoid arthritis (joints), Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE, multiple organs), Rheumatic fever (heart valves after strep)
  • Immune complex disease — antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues → chronic inflammation (mechanism in RA, lupus)

Allergy & Hypersensitivity

ImmediateDelayed
SpeedMinutesHours–days
MediatorIgE + mast cells → histamineT cells (cell-mediated)

Immediate mechanism: First exposure → IgE made → IgE binds mast cells → 2nd exposure: allergen crosslinks IgE → mast cell degranulation → histamine release → symptoms.

Tumor Immunology

  • Tumor cells may express unusual antigens (embryonic antigens or viral antigens)
  • Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) recognize tumor antigens on MHC I → kill tumor cells
  • NK cells (innate) kill cells that have lost MHC I expression (common in cancer) — "missing self" hypothesis
  • Both use perforins (punch holes in membrane) and granzymes (trigger apoptosis)
  • Cancer immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) work by unleashing CD8+ T cells against tumors
