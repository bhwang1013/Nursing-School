Part 1: ECG & the Cardiac Cycle (Lab 10)

P-R Interval

Time from start of P wave (atrial depolarization) to start of QRS complex.

QRS Duration

Ventricular depolarization — ventricles contracting.

T Wave

Ventricular repolarization — ventricles relaxing.

R-R Interval

Time between two consecutive R peaks = one complete cardiac cycle.

HR = 1 ÷ R-R interval (in minutes)
Cardiac Output
CO = HR × Stroke Volume

Example: 40 BPM × 0.125 L = 5.0 L/min

Nervous System Control

Sympathetic → increases HR
Parasympathetic (vagus) → decreases HR
At rest: vagal tone dominates

Part 2: Respiratory Volumes & Capacities (Lab 12)

VT — Tidal Volume

Volume of air in/out during one normal breath.

IRV — Inspiratory Reserve Volume

Extra air you can inhale after a normal breath in.

ERV — Expiratory Reserve Volume

Extra air you can exhale after a normal breath out.

RV — Residual Volume

Air remaining after a maximal exhalation. Cannot be measured by spirometry.

Capacities (combinations of volumes)

CapacityFormula

Vital Capacity (VC)IRV + ERV + VT

Part 3: Study Questions & Answers

How does the nervous system control heart rate?

Sympathetic nerves increase HR. Parasympathetic (vagus nerve) decreases HR. At rest, vagal tone dominates. During exercise, sympathetic activity increases and vagal activity decreases, raising HR.

What happens to the R-R interval and HR after exercise?

HR increases after exercise. Since the cardiac cycle shortens, the R-R interval decreases.

Is pulse amplitude smaller or larger immediately after whole-body exercise?

Smaller. The sympathetic nervous system causes vasoconstriction in the skin and extremities to redirect blood flow to exercising skeletal muscles.

What happens to blood volume pulse amplitude during recovery from hand exercise?

Increases. Local vasodilation opens sphincters to bring more arterial blood in — clearing CO₂, replenishing O₂, and restoring pH in fatigued muscles.

Calculate CO: HR = 40 BPM, SV = 0.125 L

CO = HR × SV = 40 × 0.125 = 5.0 L/min

When can breath be held the longest?

After a full inhalation — maximizes O₂ supply and stretches lung receptors, temporarily suppressing the urge to breathe.

What happens to breathing with hyperventilation?

Hyperventilation blows off CO₂, lowering blood PCO₂. Since the medulla triggers breathing in response to rising PCO₂, you can hold your breath much longer after hyperventilating.

How does HR change during the respiratory cycle?

Respiratory sinus arrhythmia: HR increases during inspiration and decreases during expiration. Inspiratory neurons inhibit vagal activity, briefly speeding the heart.

What is the effect of the vagus nerve on the heart?

The vagus nerve (parasympathetic) decreases heart rate. When inhibited during inspiration, HR briefly increases.

Why can't RV be measured by spirometry?

Spirometers measure air moving in and out. RV stays in the lungs after maximal exhalation — it never passes through the spirometer.

In quiet breathing, why is expiration mainly passive?

Inspiration is active (diaphragm contracts). Expiration relies on elastic recoil of the lungs — no muscles needed. Expiratory flow is therefore slower and more gradual.

What happens physiologically during an asthma attack?

Airways become inflamed and constricted, filled with mucus. Airway resistance increases → dyspnea, wheezing, and significantly reduced FEV1 (Forced Expired Volume in 1 second).

What factors cause differences in pulmonary parameters between people?

Sex, height, age, fitness level, and smoking history all affect lung volumes and capacities.
