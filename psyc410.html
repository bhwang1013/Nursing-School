📖 Study Guide

Criteria for Abnormality (4 D's)

Deviance – behavior that deviates from social/cultural norms (NOT "conformity")
Distress – personal suffering or discomfort
Dysfunction – impairs daily functioning
Dangerousness – risk of harm to self or others

⚠️ "Social conformity" is NOT a criterion — social deviance is. Know this for the exam!

Why No Single Definition Is Sufficient

No one criterion alone captures all cases of abnormality. Behaviors can be statistically rare but not disordered (e.g., genius IQ), or culturally deviant in one culture but not another. The criteria often overlap and must be considered together.

Historical Models

Biological Model: Mental illness from physical causes — brain chemistry, genetics, neurology
Psychodynamic Model: Unconscious conflicts (Freud); id, ego, superego; defense mechanisms
Behavioral/Learning Model: Abnormality learned via conditioning and reinforcement
Cognitive Model: Distorted thinking patterns cause psychological problems
Humanistic Model: Failure to achieve self-actualization; person-centered therapy
Sociocultural Model: Social and cultural factors shape behavior and diagnosis

Biopsychosocial Model

Integrates biological (genetics, brain), psychological (thoughts, emotions), and social (culture, relationships) factors. The diathesis-stress model is part of this: vulnerability (diathesis) + environmental stressors → disorder.

Key Freudian Concepts

StructurePrincipleDescription
IdPleasure principlePrimitive drives, immediate gratification, unconscious
EgoReality principleMediates between id and superego, rational
SuperegoMorality principleInternalized values, conscience, ideals

Defense Mechanisms

Repression: Blocking painful memories from consciousness
Displacement: Redirecting emotions to a safer target (e.g., anger at boss → kick the dog)
Projection: Attributing own unacceptable feelings to others
Sublimation: Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities
Rationalization: Creating logical explanations for unacceptable behavior
Reaction Formation: Acting opposite to true feelings

Prevalence Data

WHO data: United States has the HIGHEST rates of diagnosable psychological disorders worldwide
US lifetime prevalence: ~46% of US adults will have a diagnosable disorder at some point in life

Sociocultural Theories

Social Causation Theory: Poverty and hardship INCREASE the risk of mental illness (poverty → disorder)
Downward Drift Theory: Mental illness causes people to drift downward economically (disorder → poverty)

Research Methods

Correlational Method: Shows ASSOCIATION between variables. Does NOT prove causation. Correlation ≠ Causation.
Experimental Method: Manipulates independent variable; random assignment allows causal conclusions
Case Study: In-depth study of single individual; not generalizable
Naturalistic Observation: Observing behavior in natural settings; no manipulation
Survey Method: Self-report data from large groups; efficient but subject to bias

Key Research Concepts

Random Assignment: Gold standard for establishing causation; randomly places participants in conditions
Independent Variable: What the researcher manipulates
Dependent Variable: What the researcher measures
Control Group: Does not receive treatment; comparison baseline
Double-Blind: Neither participant nor researcher knows condition assignment

Research Ethics (IRB)

Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) require: Informed consent, confidentiality, right to withdraw, debriefing, and protection from harm. These are mandatory requirements — not optional.

Classical vs. Operant Conditioning

TypeKey FigureMechanismExample
ClassicalPavlovNeutral stimulus paired with unconditioned stimulus → conditioned responseBell → salivation
OperantSkinnerBehavior shaped by consequences (reinforcement/punishment)Lever press → food
Positive Reinforcement: Add a pleasant stimulus to INCREASE behavior
Negative Reinforcement: Remove an unpleasant stimulus to INCREASE behavior (≠ punishment!)

Reliability vs. Validity

Reliability: Consistency — same results across repeated administrations
Validity: Accuracy — test actually measures what it claims to measure

A test can be reliable without being valid (consistently wrong), but cannot be valid without being reliable.

Types of Psychological Tests

Objective Tests: Structured, standardized; MMPI (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory)
Projective Tests: Unstructured stimuli; reveal unconscious; Rorschach Inkblot Test, TAT
Neuropsychological Tests: Assess brain function and damage
Intelligence Tests: IQ measurement; Wechsler scales

DSM-5 Overview

Nature: Descriptive and categorical (NOT explanatory/etiological)
Organization: 20 general categories of mental disorders
Strengths: Common language for clinicians, research tool, guides treatment
Limitations: Overdiagnosis risk, cultural bias, no etiology, boundary issues between categories

Assessment Methods

Clinical Interview: Structured or unstructured; gathers history and presenting concerns
Mental Status Exam: Evaluates appearance, mood, speech, cognition
Behavioral Assessment: Observing behavior in context

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Core feature: Excessive, chronic worry across multiple life domains (NOT situation-specific)
Duration: ≥6 months
Symptoms: Restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulties, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance

Panic Disorder

Core: Repeated UNEXPECTED panic attacks + ≥1 month persistent fear of recurrence
Lifetime prevalence: ~5.1% in US
Panic attack symptoms: Palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness
Agoraphobia: Fear of places/situations where escape may be difficult; can co-occur with panic disorder

OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder)

Obsessions: Intrusive, unwanted, recurring thoughts/images/urges (ego-dystonic)
Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety from obsessions
Best treatment: ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) + CBT — more effective than medication alone

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)

Core: Preoccupation with a perceived or exaggerated physical flaw (not an actual disfigurement)
Classified under: OCD-related disorders in DSM-5
Distinguish from: Social phobia (fear of scrutiny) and health anxiety (fear of illness)

ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention)

Gradually exposes patient to feared stimuli/obsessions while preventing the compulsive response. Combined with CBT, produces greater and more lasting benefits than medication alone. Gold standard for OCD.

⚠️ Exam Alert: Common Mistakes
  • ASD is NOT diagnosed if symptoms last only 2 days (minimum 3 days)
  • PTSD cannot be diagnosed within the first month after trauma
  • Adjustment disorder onset must be within 3 months of stressor (not 1 month)
  • PTSD distinguished from ASD primarily by DURATION (not type of symptoms)

Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic Nervous System

Sympathetic (SNS): "Fight or flight" — activates stress response; increases heart rate, releases cortisol, dilates pupils
Parasympathetic (PNS): "Rest and digest" — calms body; decreases heart rate, promotes digestion

GAS — General Adaptation Syndrome (Hans Selye)

1. Alarm Stage: Initial response — SNS activation, "fight or flight"
2. Resistance Stage: Body adapts and attempts to cope with ongoing stressor
3. Exhaustion Stage: Resources depleted; vulnerability to illness and breakdown

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Lifetime prevalence: ~20.6% of US adults
Duration: ≥2 weeks of 5+ depressive symptoms
Core symptoms: Depressed mood, anhedonia (loss of pleasure), fatigue, worthlessness, sleep/appetite changes, concentration problems, suicidal ideation
Dysthymia (PDD): Persistent Depressive Disorder — chronic depression ≥2 years (milder but longer)

Bipolar Disorders

⚠️ Remember: Bipolar II is NOT milder Bipolar I

Bipolar II requires HYPOMANIA + DEPRESSION. Bipolar I requires at least ONE FULL MANIC EPISODE. The absence of full mania defines Bipolar II.

Suicide

FactDetails
Attempts vs. DeathsWomen attempt more; Men die more (more lethal methods)
Risk factorsPrevious attempts, hopelessness, social isolation, substance use, mental illness, access to means
Protective factorsSocial support, family connectedness, religious beliefs, access to mental health care

Treatments for Mood Disorders

SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors): First-line; fewer dangerous side effects, better tolerated than tricyclics
Tricyclics: Older antidepressants; effective but more side effects, lethal in overdose
CBT: Challenges negative thought patterns; effective for depression and anxiety
Mood Stabilizers (Lithium): Primary treatment for bipolar disorder

Causes of MDD — Multiple Perspectives

Biological: Serotonin/norepinephrine dysregulation, genetic predisposition, HPA axis dysfunction
Psychological: Cognitive distortions (Beck's triad: negative views of self, world, future); learned helplessness
Social/Environmental: Life stressors, social support deficits, trauma, socioeconomic factors

📝 Essay Prompts & Outlines

2 short essays worth 20 points each (40 pts total) — Click to expand sample outline

Essay 1: Define abnormal behavior and explain why no single definition is sufficient.

▼
Intro: Define abnormality as deviation from statistical norm, cultural norms, or optimal functioning. Note no universally accepted definition.
Point 1 — 4 D's Criteria: Deviance, Distress, Dysfunction, Dangerousness. Each alone is insufficient.
Why deviance alone fails: Some statistically rare behaviors (genius, athleticism) are not disorders. Cultural relativity — deviance varies by culture.
Why distress alone fails: Some disorders cause no subjective distress (e.g., antisocial personality). Some distress is normal (grief).
Why dysfunction alone fails: Dysfunction is context-dependent. A phobia of heights may not impair a desk worker.
Why dangerousness alone fails: Most people with mental disorders are not dangerous; danger can exist without disorder.
Conclusion: No single criterion captures all cases. A combination of criteria provides the most comprehensive picture. The DSM-5 uses descriptive criteria across domains rather than relying on one definition.

Essay 2: Compare and contrast panic disorder and agoraphobia. Can they co-occur?

▼
Define Panic Disorder: Repeated unexpected panic attacks + ≥1 month of persistent fear/concern about future attacks or behavioral change. Lifetime prevalence ~5.1%.
Panic Attack Symptoms: Sudden surge of intense fear with physical symptoms: palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, derealization.
Define Agoraphobia: Fear/avoidance of situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable during a panic attack — e.g., crowds, open spaces, public transit, being outside alone.
Key Difference: Panic disorder = fear of the panic attacks themselves. Agoraphobia = fear of situations associated with or following panic attacks. They are separate DSM-5 diagnoses.
Co-occurrence: YES — they commonly co-occur. Many individuals develop agoraphobia AS A RESULT of panic disorder (fear of having attacks in public → avoidance). However, either can occur independently in DSM-5.
Treatment: CBT, exposure therapy; SSRIs/SNRIs for pharmacological treatment.
Conclusion: Though related, panic disorder and agoraphobia are distinct diagnoses in DSM-5. Their frequent co-occurrence reflects how anxiety disorders can compound over time.

Essay 3: What are the strengths and limitations of the DSM-5?

▼
Overview: DSM-5 = Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition. Descriptive, categorical system. 20 general categories.
Strength 1 — Common Language: Provides standardized criteria enabling communication among clinicians and researchers across settings.
Strength 2 — Research Tool: Enables consistent research across studies; facilitates epidemiological data collection.
Strength 3 — Treatment Planning: Diagnosis guides treatment selection and insurance reimbursement.
Limitation 1 — Not Explanatory: DSM describes what behaviors constitute a disorder but does NOT explain WHY they occur (no etiology).
Limitation 2 — Cultural Bias: Criteria developed primarily from Western perspectives; some diagnoses may not translate cross-culturally.
Limitation 3 — Overdiagnosis Risk: Expanding diagnostic categories may medicalize normal human experience (e.g., grief, shyness).
Limitation 4 — Categorical vs. Dimensional: Mental health exists on a continuum; rigid categories may not reflect clinical reality.
Conclusion: The DSM-5 is an essential but imperfect tool. Its value lies in standardization; its limitations remind clinicians to treat the whole person, not just the diagnosis.

Essay 4: Explain the causes of Major Depressive Disorder using multiple perspectives.

▼
Intro: MDD is a complex disorder with ~20.6% lifetime prevalence in US adults. No single cause; best understood through the biopsychosocial model.
Biological Perspective: Monoamine hypothesis — low serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine. Genetic predisposition (heritability ~40%). HPA axis dysregulation → cortisol elevation. Structural brain changes (reduced hippocampal volume).
Psychological Perspective: Beck's Cognitive Triad — negative views of self ("I'm worthless"), the world ("everything is hopeless"), and the future ("nothing will improve"). Learned helplessness (Seligman) — belief that one has no control. Behavioral withdrawal reduces positive reinforcement.
Social/Environmental Perspective: Life stressors — loss, trauma, chronic illness. Social isolation and lack of support. Socioeconomic factors (poverty, unemployment). Social causation: hardship increases risk.
Integrating with Diathesis-Stress: Biological vulnerability (diathesis) + environmental stressors → MDD onset. Neither alone is sufficient in many cases.
Treatment Implications: Biological → SSRIs/SNRIs; Psychological → CBT; Social → social support, community resources. Best outcomes with combined approaches.
Conclusion: MDD arises from the interplay of biological predispositions, cognitive patterns, and social/environmental stressors. Effective treatment requires addressing all three domains.
